The Double Pendulum

The double pendulum is a fascinating Classical Mechanics problem. To create a double pendulum, one affixes a pendulum to the bob of another pendulum. In this, one may consider the motion of the second bob relative to the first (kind of boring really), or consider the motion of the second bob relative to the fixed contact point of the first pendulum. Modeling this system involves solving coupled second order ordinary differential equations, myriad applications of trigonometric functions and identities, sophisticated numerical approximation methods, and power computational tools. Leveraging all of these resources, I was able to derive formulas that describe the motion of a double pendulum and lend themselves well to numerical approximation; computationally generate data that accurately models that motion; and create an elegant visualization of the evolution of a double pendulum system. Included here are several examples of the sensitive, chaotic nature of the double pendulum, along with an in depth analysis of the physics and mathematics used to create them. If you are interested in an in depth analysis, take a look at my report included in this page. I will briefly address the key ideas here, as follows:

The Physics: Energy and Lagrange

As with many physical models in classical mechanics, we begin with the analysis of the system by considering it's energy states. While energy conservation is a staple in system modeling, they do not necessarily predict the evolution of a system over time. In this case, I used a more sophisticated analytical tool, the Euler-Lagrange equation. In the case of a mechanical system such as this, the Euler-Lagrange equation is simply the difference between the kinetic and potential energies. Properties of the Euler-Lagrange equation allows us to relate its derivatives and partial derivatives in different variables together, in such a way that creates a set of time-dependant differential equations. With this, we are well on our way to creating a set of purely time-dependant functions that perfectly describe the state of the system; that is, we could drop any arbitrary time value into the functions, and get a perfectly accurate description of the state at that time. Savvy readers may know that this pursuit will collapse, quickly.

The Mathematics: Coupled Differential Equations, Changes in Variables, and the Runge-Kutta Methods

By solving the Euler-Lagrange equation, I was able to create two coupled second order ordinary differential equations. Here we run into a problem: these coupled ODEs cannot be de-coupled. Ultimately, the problem is generally non-analytic (there may be some initial conditions, even non-trivial ones, that generate equations that can be solved algebraically, by hand. I am after something more general than that). Fortunately, I have more tools than just those for solving differential equations.

At its core, Calculus is built upon the idea of approximating a function as a linear function. While further analysis of this approximation allows us to move back into the realm of analytic certainty, the concept of approximating functions as simpler ones still bears fruit when we cannot backtrack to algebraic analysis. The simplest numerical approximation tool available to us for a problem like this is the forward Euler method: we use the information from the differential equations to create a linear approximation of the system. Since the approximation can diverge wildly from the true state of the system, we use only small steps in the linear approximation to create new approximations further into the evolution of the system. While we have a tool, we can do better. The Runge-Kutta method goes one (or more!) step further by approximating the system with a quadratic equation instead of a linear equation. Careful use of the Taylor Expansion about points at various locations relative to the beginning and end of the approximating step forward shows that we can create approximating quadratic equations, with surprisingly small error, to step through the evolution of our system. Customarily, and in this particular case, we use the 4th Order Runge-Kutta method; the order here referring to the order of the error term remaining in the approximation quadratic function. In this case, the error is proportional to the step size raised to the 4th power. With small steps, the error shrinks at a quartic rate. While there are higher order methods available, they become harder to work with as well; the 4th Order approximation is a reasonable balance between accuracy and simplicity.
